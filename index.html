Teacher Practice Guide

Empowering Informed, Active & Critical Global Citizens

A practical guide aligned to the NSW Human Society and its Environment K–6 Syllabus (2024), implemented from 2027, supporting teachers to inspire students as environmental and social change agents.

About This Guide

This guide is designed for primary teachers in New South Wales who are preparing to implement the new Human Society and its Environment K–6 Syllabus (2024) from 2027. It addresses a central question: how can we, as educators, empower children to become informed, active, and critical global citizens capable of taking meaningful action on the social and environmental issues that shape their world?

The guide is structured around two interconnected parts. The first explores why this work matters — drawing on research literature, the Australian Curriculum, and the new HSIE syllabus to build a compelling case for citizenship education in the primary years. The second part focuses on how teachers can achieve this in practice, with particular emphasis on inquiry-based learning, geographical and historical thinking, and the safe, responsible use of digital technologies.

Throughout the guide, you will find references to the new HSIE syllabus outcomes, focus areas, and content groups. Practical strategies are offered for every stage from Early Stage 1 through to Stage 3, ensuring that the ideas presented here are actionable across the full K–6 spectrum. The guide also includes a dedicated Quicklinks tab for rapid access to key syllabus documents, curriculum resources, and professional learning materials.

Who is this guide for? Classroom teachers, instructional leaders, and pre-service educators in NSW primary schools who want to deepen their understanding of how the HSIE K–6 Syllabus (2024) can be harnessed to foster active, critical citizenship.

Begin by exploring the Why It Matters tab to understand the rationale, then move through the syllabus overview and practical strategies. The Quicklinks tab provides fast access to essential resources, and the References tab lists all cited works.

Why Empower Informed, Active and Critical Global Citizens?

The call to develop informed, active, and critical citizens sits at the heart of the new HSIE syllabus and reflects a growing international consensus about the purpose of education in the twenty-first century. As the NSW Education Standards Authority articulates in the syllabus rationale, students are expected to "become active, confident, engaged and valued members of Australia's diverse community" and to "contribute to a socially just and sustainable future" (NESA, 2024a). Empowering our students is not superficial aspiration, it is the central to the HSIE syllabus.

Breaking Down the Key Concepts

Informed citizens possess the geographical and historical knowledge needed to understand the world. They can locate places, interpret data, and draw conclusions from evidence. In the HSIE syllabus, this is developed through outcomes such as HS2-GEO-01, where students explain how people care for Australia's environments using geographical information, and HS3-HIS-01, where they examine the development of Australian colonies and the nation using sources as evidence (NESA, 2024c).

Active citizens move beyond knowledge to participation. They engage in their communities, contribute to decision-making, and take action on issues that matter. The syllabus supports this through content that asks students to conduct surveys and design responses for the needs of the community (Stage 2), propose strategies to manage local environments (Stage 3), and identifying and participating in caring for places (Early Stage 1 and Stage 1 respectively). This aligns with Oxfam's (2015) framework for global citizenship, which emphasises that learners should be equipped not just to understand the world but to act within it responsibly and collaboratively.

Critical citizens question assumptions, evaluate sources, and consider multiple perspectives. Australian researchers have emphasised that civics and citizenship education must move beyond simple knowledge of government structures toward a critical engagement with social justice issues (Tudball & Henderson, 2014). This critical orientation is precisely what the new HSIE syllabus fosters, particularly at Stage 3, where students examine diverse perspectives on the past, explain the significance of Aboriginal Languages revival, and research shared experiences of Indigenous peoples internationally (NESA, 2024b).

Key insight: The syllabus acts as "a mirror to reflect a student's personal experiences and a window through which to view the world at different times and places" (NESA, 2024a). This dual function is what makes the HSIE learning area uniquely positioned to develop global citizenship in the primary years.

Research consistently shows that when children are given opportunities to engage with real-world issues, their motivation and depth of learning increase significantly. Murdoch (2015) argued that enquiry-based approaches, where students pose questions, investigate sources, and draw their own conclusions, are particularly effective in fostering the critical thinking and agency required for active citizenship. The teacher's role, then, is not simply to transmit information but to create the conditions in which students can discover their own capacity to effect change.

The HSIE K–6 Syllabus (2024): A Framework for Citizenship

The NSW Human Society and its Environment K–6 Syllabus (2024), to be implemented from 2027, represents a significant evolution from the previous separate Geography and History syllabuses. It integrates both disciplines under a unified framework while maintaining their distinct focus areas and ways of thinking. Understanding this structure is essential for teachers who wish to plan effectively for citizenship education.

Organisation of the Syllabus

The syllabus is organised around two strands: Geography and History, each containing four focus areas that progress across the stages of learning. The Geography focus areas move from "People are connected to places" (Early Stage 1) through to "Geographical information is used to plan for sustainable futures" (Stage 3). The History focus areas progress from "People have lived in the past" (Early Stage 1) to "Historical sources present perspectives on the past" (Stage 3). Across all stages, the syllabus emphasises "Applying knowledge, understanding and skills through interrelated practices" (NESA, 2024b).

Key Outcomes That Support Citizenship

Several outcomes across the stages directly support the development of informed, active, and critical citizens:

  • ES1 HSE-GEO-01 — Identifies and locates places people connect with, using geographical information. This foundational outcome begins the journey of understanding connection to place.
  • S1 HS1-GEO-01 — Describes ways people connect to and care for places, water environments and each other. Here, the active dimension of citizenship emerges as students learn about care and responsibility.
  • S2 HS2-GEO-01 — Explains how people care for Australia's environments and participate in Australian society. The concept of participation becomes explicit, linking environmental stewardship with civic engagement.
  • S3 HS3-GEO-01 — Examines global citizenship and how people organise, protect and sustainably use the environment. This outcome directly names global citizenship as a focus, representing the culmination of the K–6 journey.
  • S3 HS3-DEM-01 — Describes the origins and features of Australia's democratic system of government and laws. The civics and citizenship dimension is made explicit at Stage 3, preparing students for active participation in democratic processes.

Cross-Curriculum Priorities and General Capabilities

The Australian Curriculum and syllabus is designed to be taught in conjunction with the cross-curriculum priorities and general capabilities outlined in the Australian Curriculum (ACARA, 2022). Three priorities are particularly relevant to citizenship education: Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Histories and Cultures, which runs through every stage of the syllabus; Sustainability, which is central to the Geography strand; and Asia and Australia's Engagement with Asia, which supports the global dimension of citizenship. The general capabilities of Critical and Creative Thinking, Ethical Understanding, and Intercultural Understanding are also deeply embedded in the content and outcomes.

Teachers programming with the new NSW HSIE syllabus could look for the natural connections between these priorities and the focus areas of NSW's syllabus. For example, when teaching Stage 2 content on "People have responsibility to care for Australia's environments" (NESA, 2024b) the Sustainability priority can be addressed by investigating Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander food practices that are sustainable.

Practical Strategies for Empowering Young Citizens

The Illawarra region offers a uniquely rich setting for empowering students as informed, active, and critical global citizens. From the escarpment to the coastline, from the heavy industry of Port Kembla to the fragile ecosystems of Lake Illawarra, local places present authentic opportunities for geographical and historical inquiry. By grounding learning in familiar landscapes and real community issues, teachers can make the abstract aims of the HSIE syllabus tangible and deeply engaging for their students.

Inquiry-Based Learning Through Local Issues

Inquiry-based learning is not merely a technique, it is the pedagogical heart of the new HSIE syllabus. When students investigate questions that arise from their own surroundings, they develop the skills of geographers, historians, and active citizens simultaneously. The Illawarra provides countless starting points: How has the coastline changed over time? Why is the steelworks located at Port Kembla? What stories do local Aboriginal sites tell about the land before European settlement? Hull and Murdoch (2023) emphasises that in an inquiry classroom the teacher's role is to ensure students "do more of the heavy lifting" of their own thinking, while the teacher remains explicit about the purpose of learning and uses direct instruction judiciously. With the Illawarra as our lab, we as teachers scaffold the way to deeper student-centric inquiry.

Example inquiry: Bluescope steelworks and its impact. A Stage 3 class investigating “How do people organise, protect and sustainably use the environment?” could develop an inquiry around the question: What positive and negative impacts has the Bluescope steelworks had on the environment and community of Port Kembla? Students could examine historical photographs of the site before industrialisation, analyse air quality data from local monitoring stations, view satellite images showing land-use change, and invite a guest speaker from the steelworks or an environmental group. They would then draw conclusions about the trade-offs involved in industrial activity and propose strategies for a more sustainable future, directly addressing outcome HS3-GEO-01.

Connecting to Real-World Issues Across Stages

The Wollongong area offers entry points for every stage of learning. Early Stage 1 students observing the natural and human features of their school grounds can extend their investigation to a nearby beach or park, describing how they care for these special places. Stage 1 classes can explore the freshwater and saltwater bodies of the region, from the streams of the escarpment to Lake Illawarra, comparing how people use and care for these water environments. Stage 2 students can examine how climate influences the settlement patterns along the Illawarra coastline, using choropleth maps and rainfall data from the Bureau of Meteorology. At Stage 3, the focus shifts to global citizenship and sustainable futures, and the steelworks inquiry above demonstrates how a local case study can illuminate universal questions about industry, environment, and community wellbeing.

Using Sources to Build Critical Thinking

The History strand of the new syllabus places significant emphasis on the use of sources as evidence for historical perpectives, and the Illawarra offers a wealth of primary material. Students can analyse photographs of the Port Kembla steelworks from the 1920s alongside contemporary images, examine newspaper articles about environmental campaigns in the region, and engage with the oral histories of local residents, including members of the Dharawal community. Teachers can support critical thinking by explicitly teaching students to ask: Who created this source and for what purpose? Whose perspective is represented? What information might be missing? When students apply these questions to sources about the development of the steel industry, they build the analytical habits that characterise critical citizens, and they come to understand that history is not a single story but a collection of perspectives.

The Wodi Wodi people are the Traditional Custodians of Dharawal Country on which Wollongong and its surrounds exists. The new HSIE syllabus requires that students engage with Aboriginal Cultures and Histories at every stage, and local connections make this requirement meaningful. Teachers might begin a unit by acknowledging Country using local land links and investigating the significance of local sites such as Mount Kembla, the Five Islands, or the Moreton Bay Figtree Park. When exploring the impact of industry, students can consider how the steelworks and associated development have affected Aboriginal people’s connection to Country over time. Partnering with local Aboriginal Elders and Community members is essential; their Cultural and Ancient Knowledges bring depth and authenticity that no textbook can replicate, and they model the respect and collaboration that underpin genuine active citizenship

Creating Written Texts to Consolidate Learning

Throughout an inquiry, students should be regularly creating written texts that help them organise their thinking and communicate their findings. After investigating Bluescope steelworks, Stage 3 students might write a persuasive letter to the local council proposing ways to balance industrial activity with environmental protection, drawing on evidence gathered during their research. Stage 2 students might create an information report on the unique features of the Illawarra escarpment, using Tier 2 and Tier 3 vocabulary as specified in the syllabus. These writing tasks are opportunities for students to deepen their understanding and to experience what it means to have an informed, articulate voice in public conversation.

From Local Action to Global Citizenship

Active citizenship begins in the local but does not end there. Once students have developed the confidence to investigate and respond to issues in their own community, they are better prepared to engage with national and global challenges. A class that has explored the environmental impact of Bluescope steelworks can connect their findings to broader questions about sustainable industry around the world, linking their local inquiry to the global citizenship outcomes of Stage 3. This progression from the known to the new is a powerful pedagogical principle, and it is one that the Illawarra environment scaffolds with exceptional richness of industry and culture. By designing learning that is rooted in the local yet connected to the global, teachers in the Wollongong region are uniquely positioned to empower the informed, active, and critical citizens that the HSIE syllabus envisions.

ICT and Digital Technologies: Safe, Responsible and Ethical Use

Digital technologies offer powerful tools for supporting inquiry-based learning and citizenship education, but their use must be guided by clear principles of safety, responsibility, and ethics. The Australian Professional Standards for Teachers require that educators model and teach the safe, responsible, and ethical use of ICT (AITSL, 2018), and this expectation is reflected in the Australian Curriculum's general capability of Digital Literacy (ACARA, 2022).

Integrating Digital Tools into HSIE

Within the HSIE syllabus, digital technologies can enhance learning in several meaningful ways. Students can use online mapping tools to explore geographical information, access digital archives to examine historical sources, and collaborate on shared documents to plan community action projects. At Stage 3, when students research "practices people can engage with to sustainably manage or protect global environments into the future" (NESA, 2024b), they might use digital presentation tools to share their findings with an authentic audience, such as the school community or a local council.

Important: Whenever students are using digital technologies for research or communication, teachers must explicitly teach skills related to evaluating online sources, protecting personal information, and engaging respectfully in digital spaces. These are not add-ons but integral components of the learning experience.

Practical Considerations for the Classroom

Before embarking on a digitally enhanced inquiry, teachers should consider the following: Are the digital tools age-appropriate and accessible to all learners? Have students been taught how to critically evaluate information found online? Are there clear guidelines in place for respectful online collaboration? Have families been informed about the digital platforms being used? Addressing these questions proactively ensures that technology serves the learning goals rather than creating unintended risks.

The ethical dimension is particularly important when students are engaging with content related to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Cultures and Histories. Teachers must ensure that any digital resources used are culturally appropriate and that students understand the significance of Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property protocols. The NESA curriculum website provides guidance on these matters, and local Aboriginal Education Consultative Groups can offer valuable advice.
